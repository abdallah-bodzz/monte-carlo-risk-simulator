Log Returns

We compute log returns: r_t = ln(P_t / P_{t−1}), not simple percentage returns. The critical property is that log returns are additive over time:

r_total = r₁ + r₂ + ··· + r_T

Simple returns compound: (1+r_total) = (1+r₁)(1+r₂)···(1+r_T). Summing them directly, as we do in Monte Carlo, would be mathematically incorrect. Log returns avoid this entirely and also distribute more symmetrically — a better fit for the distributions we use.

Cholesky Decomposition — Correlated Assets

Assets are correlated. SPY and AAPL move together; GLD moves independently of equities. Simulating each asset with independent random draws ignores this and misstates portfolio risk — usually overstating it.

We estimate the historical covariance matrix Σ from daily log returns, then decompose it: Σ = L · Lᵀ (Cholesky factorisation). For independent standard-normal draws Z:

X = L · Z    ⟹    X ~ N(0, Σ)

X carries exactly the cross-asset correlation structure from historical data. This is the mathematical core of the engine — the reason the risk estimate is correct rather than just plausible.

Value at Risk (VaR)

VaR_c = −Quantile(R, 1 − c)

"With probability c, the portfolio will not lose more than VaR over the horizon." At 95%: only 5% of the 10,000 simulated paths produce a worse result. VaR is a threshold — it says nothing about the magnitude of losses beyond it.

Expected Shortfall (ES) — the better metric

ES_c = −E[ R  |  R ≤ −VaR_c ]

"Given we ARE in the worst (1−c)% of scenarios, what is the average loss?" ES is always ≥ VaR. The ratio ES/VaR quantifies tail heaviness. Basel III (2016) replaced VaR with ES as the regulatory standard precisely because ES captures how bad the tail is — not just where it starts.

Student-t — Fat Tails

Z ~ t(ν=5)     standardised: Z / √(ν/(ν−2))

Real market returns have more extreme moves than a Gaussian predicts. Student-t with ν=5 has roughly twice the tail weight of Normal. We standardise draws (divide by √(ν/(ν−2)) so Var=1) before the Cholesky step — the correlation structure is applied identically either way. As ν→∞, Student-t converges to Normal.

Annualisation

μ_annual = μ_daily × 252    ·    Σ_annual = Σ_daily × 252

Variance scales linearly with time; volatility (std dev) scales with √252. We estimate μ and Σ from 2 years of daily log returns, scale to annualised figures for display, but the simulation uses raw daily parameters and sums T days directly — no √T shortcuts that compound error.
